# Azle Drainage Voice Profile

## Voice Summary

Sounds like getting a text from the guy who actually digs the trenches -- not a marketing department, not a franchise script, not a chatbot. Blue-collar authentic with quiet expertise. Bert talks to homeowners the same way he'd talk to a neighbor leaning over the fence -- direct, helpful, no BS, and occasionally funny. You trust him because he doesn't try to impress you. He just tells you what's going on with your yard.

## Core Personality Traits

- **Blue-collar expert:** Knows drainage inside and out but explains it like he's showing you in person. Points at things. Uses plain words. Never lectures. The expertise shows in the specificity, not the vocabulary.
- **Honest to a fault:** Tells you what's actually wrong, what it'll actually cost, and whether you actually need it. Won't sell you something you don't need. Won't sugarcoat a problem either.
- **Helpful neighbor energy:** The guy on your street who happens to know everything about water and dirt. He'd walk over and look at your yard for free just because you asked. Not transactional -- genuinely wants to help.
- **Quietly confident:** Doesn't need to brag. Lets the work speak. But when he says "I can fix that," you believe him because he's specific about how and why.
- **Local pride:** Azle is home, not a market. North Texas clay isn't a sales angle -- it's the soil he's worked in his whole career. He knows these neighborhoods, these yards, this weather.

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Formal <> Casual | Very casual | Contractions always. Fragments OK. Sentence starters like "And" and "But" are fine. Reads like a text message, not a brochure. |
| Serious <> Playful | Serious with dry humor | The work is serious (foundations, flooding, real money). But Bert has a dry wit -- "March in North Texas, am I right?" Not joke-cracking, just human. |
| Reserved <> Bold | Bold but not loud | Makes strong claims backed by specifics. "Your landscaper can't fix this." Doesn't hedge. But never aggressive or confrontational -- matter-of-fact. |
| Simple <> Sophisticated | Simple words, smart ideas | Uses "dirt" not "substrate." "Slope" not "grade differential." The thinking is sophisticated (clay soil engineering, hydrology) but the words are 8th-grade reading level. |
| Warm <> Direct | Direct with warmth underneath | Leads with facts and straight talk. But the warmth is there -- he genuinely cares about your house, your yard, your foundation. It comes through in the offers: "I'll come look at it myself. No charge." |

## Vocabulary

**Words/phrases to USE:**
- "That's all we do" -- signature positioning line
- "Text us a photo" -- signature CTA, low-friction
- "I'll come look at it myself" -- personal commitment
- "No charge, no pitch" -- trust builder
- "Here's the thing" / "Here's why" -- transition into expertise
- "North Texas clay" -- mechanism language, use often
- "See that?" / "Look at this" -- showing, not telling
- "It showed" -- understated criticism of bad work
- "Straight answer" -- what Bert gives you
- "Fix it for good" / "fix it right the first time" -- permanence promise
- "Standing water" -- problem language homeowners use
- "Your yard" / "your property" / "your foundation" -- make it personal
- "Text me at (682) 516-6797" -- direct, personal CTA
- City names: Azle, Weatherford, Springtown, Fort Worth -- local proof

**Words/phrases to AVOID:**
- "We're committed to excellence" -- corporate garbage
- "Don't hesitate to contact us" -- nobody talks like this
- "Comprehensive solutions" / "tailored approach" -- marketing speak
- "State-of-the-art" / "cutting-edge" -- oversells a trench
- "Your satisfaction is our priority" -- empty
- "Premium" / "luxury" -- this is drainage, not a spa
- "Leverage" / "utilize" / "implement" -- use "use"
- "Passionate about drainage" -- nobody is passionate about drainage. Bert is good at it. That's better.
- "Industry-leading" / "best-in-class" -- prove it, don't claim it
- "Schedule your consultation today!" -- too corporate for a CTA
- "We would love the opportunity" -- begging energy
- "Transform your outdoor space" -- that's a landscaper line
- Exclamation marks in clusters (!!!) -- one is fine, rarely

**Jargon level:** Translated. Bert knows the technical terms but always translates them. "Perforated pipe" becomes "pipe with holes that lets water in." "Grading" becomes "making the ground slope the right way." He'll use the real term once, then explain it.

**Profanity:** Never in copy. Bert is clean-spoken. The roughness comes from directness, not language.

## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Short to medium. 5-15 words is the sweet spot. Use fragments for emphasis: "Wrong materials. Wrong slope. Wrong approach." Longest sentences are lists or explanations, and even those stay under 25 words.

**Paragraphs:** 1-3 sentences. Lots of white space. One idea per paragraph. When in doubt, hit enter. Dense blocks of text feel corporate.

**Openings:** Three patterns that work:
1. Bold statement: "Your landscaper can't fix your drainage."
2. Proof/story: "Last week a homeowner in Azle called us after three different landscapers couldn't fix her drainage."
3. Problem trigger: "Still watching water pool every time it rains?"

Never open with: "Welcome to Azle Drainage Solutions!" or "At Azle Drainage, we believe..."

**Closings:** Simple ask. Never a hard sell. Patterns:
1. "Text us at (682) 516-6797 or fill out the form."
2. "I'll come look at it myself. No charge."
3. "Text us a photo. We'll text you back with what it'll take to fix it."

Never close with: "Don't wait -- call now before it's too late!" or "Limited spots available!"

**Formatting:** Minimal. Short paragraphs do the structural work. Bold sparingly -- only for emphasis on a key phrase, not for decoration. No emojis in ads or web copy. One emoji max in social posts, and only if it's relevant (rain cloud, not sparkles).

## POV & Address

**First person:** Mix of "I" (Bert personally) and "We" (the company/crew). Use "I" when making personal commitments ("I'll come look at it myself") and "We" when talking about the work ("We build systems engineered for Texas clay"). Never use "one" or passive voice ("It is recommended that...").

**Reader address:** "You" -- always direct. "Your yard," "your foundation," "your property." Make it personal and specific. Occasionally "homeowners in Azle" for geo-targeting context, but default to "you."

**Relationship stance:** Expert neighbor. Not a teacher (too formal), not a peer (Bert genuinely knows more about drainage), not a guru (too pretentious). He's the neighbor who happens to be the best drainage contractor in the area. You trust him because he's been doing this forever, he's local, and he shows up personally.

## Example Phrases

**On-brand (sounds like Bert):**
- "Drainage is all we do. Every truck, every tool, every hour of our day goes into solving water problems."
- "See that gray stuff? That's clay. It eats standard drainage."
- "I'm Bert. I started Azle Drainage because every time I saw a flooded yard, the homeowner had called a landscaper who 'also does drainage.' And it showed."
- "We fixed it in a day. She texted us after the next rain: 'First time in four years my patio isn't underwater.'"
- "Text us a photo. We'll text you back with what it'll take to fix it."
- "That's what drainage is supposed to do."
- "Just pipe in dirt and hopes. That's what you get when drainage is someone's side gig."
- "No charge, no obligation. Just a straight answer from a guy who works in this dirt every day."

**Off-brand (doesn't sound like Bert):**
- "Our team of dedicated professionals is committed to providing superior drainage solutions for your residential property." -- Corporate, distant, could be any company anywhere
- "Don't let water damage destroy your home! Act NOW before it's too late!!!" -- Fear-mongering, fake urgency, exclamation abuse
- "We leverage industry-leading techniques to deliver comprehensive water management systems." -- Jargon, buzzwords, says nothing
- "At Azle Drainage Solutions, we're passionate about helping homeowners achieve optimal yard drainage." -- Nobody is passionate about drainage. Just fix the yard.
- "Ready to transform your outdoor living experience? Schedule a complimentary consultation with our experts today!" -- Sounds like a landscaper selling a pergola, not a drainage contractor fixing a flood
- "As North Texas's premier drainage provider, we pride ourselves on exceeding expectations." -- Empty, unprovable, forgettable

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Reference specific North Texas details -- clay soil, spring storms, flash floods, the neighborhoods by name
- Sound like Bert talking to a neighbor, not a brand talking to a "target audience"
- Use real numbers, real timelines, real job details when possible ("fixed it in a day," "$4K drain destroyed by clay")
- Make the CTA easy and low-friction -- "text us a photo" beats "schedule a consultation"
- Let the work speak -- before/after photos, rain day footage, tear-out reveals
- Acknowledge the homeowner's frustration without milking it
- Use contractions, always (we're, that's, don't, won't, it's, you're, he's)
- Keep paragraphs short -- if it looks like a wall of text, break it up
- End with a simple ask, never a hard sell

**DON'T:**
- Sound like a franchise, a call center, or a marketing department
- Use fake urgency ("Only 3 spots left this month!")
- Over-promise ("We guarantee your yard will never flood again!")
- Trash-talk competitors by name -- critique the approach, not the company
- Use stock photos. Ever. People can tell.
- Write long paragraphs -- if it looks corporate, it reads corporate
- Add filler words to sound professional ("utilize," "facilitate," "implement")
- Apologize for selling -- Bert solves a real problem and deserves to be paid for it
- Use emojis in ads or website copy
- Sound like AI-generated content -- if a sentence could appear on any drainage website in America, rewrite it
