Last email I talked about French drains. They're great at handling water that sits in the soil. But some yards have a different problem: water moving fast across the surface.

That's where catch basins come in.

A catch basin is basically a box in the ground with a grate on top. Water flows across the surface, drops into the box, and gets routed through a pipe to your discharge point.

Think of it like a storm drain in a parking lot. Same idea, smaller scale.

You probably need a catch basin when:

  • Water runs across your patio or driveway after rain
  • There's a low spot where water funnels from multiple directions
  • Roof downspouts dump water in one spot and it has nowhere to go
  • Your neighbor's yard drains onto yours and creates a rushing flow

How catch basins and French drains work together:

On a lot of our jobs, we install both. The catch basin grabs the fast-moving surface water. The French drain handles the slow-seeping underground water. The catch basin ties into the French drain's pipe network, and everything flows to the same outlet.

It's a system, not just a single fix.

When I walk your yard, I'm looking at both problems — surface water AND subsurface water. Most homeowners only notice the surface stuff (the puddles). The subsurface water is what's doing the real damage to your foundation.

Both need to be addressed. That's why a proper assessment matters.
