Here's a simple question: where do your downspouts dump water?

If the answer is "right next to the foundation" — you have a problem that's probably costing you money right now.

Your roof collects every drop of rain that falls on it. On a 2,000 square foot home, a 1-inch rain produces over 1,200 gallons of water. That water funnels down your gutters, through the downspouts, and out the bottom.

If the bottom of that downspout is sitting next to your house, you're pouring 1,200 gallons against your foundation every time it rains. In North Texas clay, that water doesn't drain away. It soaks in, swells the clay, and pushes on your slab.

The fix is called downspout burial (or extension). We connect a pipe to the bottom of your downspout, bury it underground, and route it away from the house to a discharge point. Usually to the curb or a pop-up emitter at least 10 feet from the foundation.

It's one of the simplest and cheapest things we do. And it's one of the most effective at protecting your foundation.

If you don't want to do anything else right now — at least get your downspouts routed away from the house. It's the single best bang-for-your-buck move in residential drainage.
