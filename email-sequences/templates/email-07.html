This one's for you if you might sell your house in the next few years. Or even if you're not sure.

When a buyer hires a home inspector, the inspector walks the yard. And they know what to look for.

Standing water. Erosion along the foundation. Soil grading that slopes toward the house instead of away. Staining on the slab. Cracks in the brick. Moisture in the crawl space.

Every one of those gets written up in the inspection report. And every one gives the buyer a reason to either negotiate your price down or walk away.

I've talked to homeowners who lost $8,000-$10,000 at the negotiating table because of drainage issues that would have cost a fraction of that to fix beforehand. The buyer's agent uses the inspection report like a shopping list of price reductions.

The other thing: foundation issues caused by poor drainage show up on the report too. If the inspector flags foundation movement, the buyer's going to want a structural engineer involved. Now you're talking about serious money and serious delays.

Fixing drainage before you sell isn't just about the yard looking nice. It's about protecting your equity.

And even if you're not selling anytime soon — the problem doesn't get cheaper to fix with time. The opposite, actually.
