Here's something most homeowners around Azle don't know.

The soil under your yard isn't regular dirt. It's clay. Dense, heavy, North Texas clay that behaves nothing like the sandy soil you'd find an hour north of here.

When it rains, this clay swells up like a sponge. It holds water instead of letting it drain through. That's why your yard turns into a pond after a storm — the water has nowhere to go.

**Preview text:** It's not bad workmanship. It's bad assumptions about North Texas soil.

---

**Body:**

If you've already had drainage work done and it didn't solve the problem — or it worked for a year and stopped — I'm not surprised.

About half the drainage jobs we do are fixing someone else's install.

Here's what usually went wrong:

**Wrong pipe.** Corrugated pipe is cheap and easy. It's also flexible, which means North Texas clay crushes it flat within a couple years. We use rigid PVC. It costs more. It lasts.

**No filter fabric.** Or the wrong fabric. Clay migrates through standard landscape fabric and packs into the gravel. Within two years, your drain is a solid block of clay pretending to be a French drain.

**No slope.** Water flows downhill. That sounds obvious, but you'd be amazed how many drain lines we pull out that were installed flat — or even tilted the wrong direction. Without proper slope, water sits in the pipe instead of flowing to the outlet.

**No outlet.** Where does the water go? Some installs just... end. Pipe goes into the ground and stops. If there's no discharge point — a pop-up emitter, a tie-in to the storm drain, a daylight outlet — the water has nowhere to go.

**Wrong assumptions about the soil.** This is the big one. Contractors who learned drainage in sandy soil use the same approach here. It doesn't work. North Texas clay is a different animal. It needs a different approach.

If any of that sounds like your situation, you're not starting from zero. You've just been dealing with someone who didn't account for the clay.

We can usually diagnose what went wrong in about 20 minutes on your property.

## Email 13 — French drains: the gold standard

**Day 168** | Phase: VALUE | Custom Graphic: No

---

**Subject line:** French drains: what they are and when you need one
- Alt A: The most common drainage fix (explained simply)
- Alt B: French drains — how they work, plain English

**Preview text:** A French drain is a trench with pipe and gravel that catches water underground and moves it away. Here's how it works.

---

**Body:**

If you've been looking into drainage, you've probably seen "French drain" a hundred times. Here's what it actually is, in plain English.

A French drain is a trench — usually 12 to 18 inches deep — with a perforated pipe (pipe with holes that lets water in) sitting on a bed of gravel. The trench is lined with filter fabric to keep the soil out. Gravel fills the rest.

Here's how it works: water in the soil seeps through the gravel, enters the pipe through the holes, and flows through the pipe to a discharge point — usually a pop-up emitter at the curb or a tie-in to the storm system.

It works underground, out of sight. Once the grass grows back, you'd never know it's there.

**When you need one:**
- Standing water in the middle of your yard that sits for days
- Water pooling along a fence line or property boundary
- Low spots where runoff collects
- Soggy areas that never fully dry out

**When you might need something else (or something in addition):**
- Water pooling right against your foundation — might need grading + a French drain
- Roof runoff creating channels in your yard — might need downspout extensions
- A specific low spot collecting water from a large area — might need a catch basin feeding into a French drain

French drains are the gold standard for residential drainage in North Texas. They handle the biggest problem — water that sits in clay soil and has nowhere to go.

But they only work if they're built for our soil. Standard installs designed for sandy or loamy soil don't hold up in clay. That's the part most people learn the hard way.

- Bert

## Email 14 — When you need a catch basin (not just a French drain)

**Day 182** | Phase: VALUE | Custom Graphic: No

---

**Subject line:** When a French drain isn't enough
- Alt A: Catch basins: the missing piece most people skip
- Alt B: Your yard might need more than a French drain

**Preview text:** A catch basin grabs surface water fast. A French drain handles what's underground. Together they're unbeatable.

---

**Body:**

Last email I talked about French drains. They're great at handling water that sits in the soil. But some yards have a different problem: water moving fast across the surface.

That's where catch basins come in.

A catch basin is basically a box in the ground with a grate on top. Water flows across the surface, drops into the box, and gets routed through a pipe to your discharge point.

Think of it like a storm drain in a parking lot. Same idea, smaller scale.

**You probably need a catch basin when:**
- Water runs across your patio or driveway after rain
- There's a low spot where water funnels from multiple directions
- Roof downspouts dump water in one spot and it has nowhere to go
