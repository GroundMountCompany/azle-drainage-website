  INSTAGRAM VERSION:

  "What even is a French drain?"

  I get this question at least once a week so here you go.

  A French drain is a trench filled with gravel and a
  perforated pipe at the bottom. Water seeps through the
  gravel, hits the pipe, and gets carried away from your
  house by gravity. That's it. No pumps, no electricity.
  Just physics.

  The reason they work so well in North Texas is because
  our clay soil won't let water drain on its own. The
  French drain creates an underground highway for the
  water -- bypasses the clay entirely.

  We dig the trench, set the grade so water flows downhill
  to the discharge point, lay the pipe, pack it with
  washed gravel, and cover it up. When it's done you
  barely see it. But next time it rains, your yard drains
  in hours instead of days.

  FACEBOOK VERSION:

  Got called out to a place in Weatherford last month.
  Homeowner told me they'd had another company install a
  French drain two years ago. Yard was still flooding.

  When we dug it up, here's what we found:

  - Pipe wasn't perforated. It was solid PVC. So water
    had no way to actually get into the drain.
  - No gravel. They'd just buried the pipe in the clay
    and backfilled with dirt.
  - Zero slope. The pipe was flat -- water was just
    sitting in it going nowhere.

  Basically it was an underground pipe that did nothing.

  We ripped it all out, dug the trench to proper grade,
  laid perforated pipe with clean washed gravel, wrapped
  it in fabric to keep the fines out, and ran it to a
  proper discharge point.

  Rained hard two weeks later. Yard drained like it's
  supposed to.

  I don't say this to trash other companies. I say it so
  you know what to look for. If someone installs a French
  drain and your yard still floods after rain, something
  went wrong. It's worth a second look.
